## Definition

A rate card is a structured list of unit prices for usage, add-ons, and services used in quoting and contracts.

## Why it matters

Rate cards keep pricing consistent across sales, renewals, and expansions. They reduce ad-hoc discounting and
confusion, especially when multiple teams need to quote the same product with the same rules.

## What a rate card does and does not solve

A rate card solves consistency. It does not solve weak packaging, poor margin discipline, or a missing pricing policy.
If the rate card exists only as a spreadsheet artifact, it can actually hide pricing leakage rather than prevent it.

The useful question is not "do we have a rate card?" The useful question is "does the rate card encode the pricing
rules the business wants sales and finance to follow?"

## Pricing implications

If your rate card is too complex, deals slow down. If it is too simple, you may underprice high-cost usage. The best
rate cards stay aligned with cost drivers, margin targets, and approval policy instead of trying to document every
exception on the page.

## Measurement tips

Track discount rates and deviations from the rate card to identify pricing leakage. If exceptions are frequent, the
problem is often a policy issue or a packaging issue rather than a quoting issue.

## Common mistakes

- Letting each rep maintain a different unofficial rate list.
- Mixing one-off services into the same line items as recurring usage.
- Allowing discount exceptions without a clear approval path.
- Updating the rate card after the fact instead of treating it as the source of truth.
