## Definition

Grandfathering means keeping some or all existing customers on an older price or package while new customers move to a
newer one. It is usually used during pricing increases, packaging changes, or contract migrations when the business
does not want every customer to absorb the new logic immediately.

## Why it matters in pricing decisions

Grandfathering matters because it changes the trade-off between revenue speed and retention risk. A full migration may
lift revenue faster, but it can also trigger more support load, renewal friction, and churn if the new package is hard
to explain or materially more expensive for legacy customers. Grandfathering slows the revenue uplift, but it can
protect trust while the business proves the new structure is worth paying for.

The stronger question is not whether grandfathering is generous. The stronger question is whether it buys time for a
cleaner transition or simply preserves weak legacy pricing for too long.

## When it helps and when it creates drag

Grandfathering usually helps when:

- a pricing increase would land before customers have seen enough new value
- contract terms or billing cycles make immediate migration feel unfair
- the product is changing package logic, not only price, and buyers need time to understand the new model
- support and success teams need a controlled transition instead of a one-day policy change

It creates drag when:

- the business keeps adding exceptions and legacy tiers with no end state
- legacy customers never see a clear upgrade path
- teams use grandfathering to avoid explaining the new value story
- the old price is so weak that margin or service quality deteriorates while the legacy cohort remains large

## Common interpretation mistakes

- Treating grandfathering as a permanent loyalty benefit instead of a temporary transition policy.
- Applying the same policy to every segment, even when enterprise contracts and self-serve renewals behave differently.
- Forgetting to define an end state, which usually creates a messy stack of legacy tiers.
- Assuming grandfathering solves churn risk when the real issue is weak product value or a confusing new package.

## Example

Imagine a SaaS product moving from a flat $99 plan to a platform fee plus usage overages. Self-serve customers who
barely use the product may react badly if the new packaging lands all at once, even if larger accounts would pay more
fairly under the new model. The business chooses to grandfather the smallest legacy accounts until renewal while moving
new buyers immediately onto the new structure. That slows near-term uplift, but it creates a cleaner rollout and avoids
forcing low-context customers through a sudden pricing rewrite.
