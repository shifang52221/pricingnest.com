## What this page is for

MRR expansion tracking tells you how much revenue growth comes from upgrades, add-ons, and other expansion behavior
rather than from new logos. It is the right page to use when the business needs a clean view of retention-led growth.

If the team does not separate expansion from churn and contraction, the growth story can look better than the actual
revenue base. That makes it harder to tell whether pricing changes are truly healthy.

## What to separate before you measure expansion

Before using expansion MRR as a decision signal, separate:

- new-logo MRR
- upgrade-driven expansion
- add-on expansion
- downgrade or contraction
- full churn

Those categories answer different questions. Mixing them together makes a healthy cohort look stronger than it is and a
weak cohort look more stable than it really is.

## Why it matters

Expansion is one of the clearest signs that customers are growing inside the product. But expansion only means
something when it is isolated from one-time fees, annual billing timing, or contract changes that inflate the number
without changing the underlying relationship.

The useful question is not just "did MRR grow?" The useful question is "did retained customers expand enough to offset
contraction and churn?"

## How to read the metric

Look at expansion by plan, segment, and cohort. If expansion is concentrated in one segment, that may be real product
health. If it is concentrated in one billing motion or one pricing exception, it may be a reporting artifact.

Expansion should also be checked alongside NRR and churn impact. A strong expansion number does not rescue a model if
downward movement is rising faster in the same period.

## Common mistakes

- Counting new logos as expansion.
- Leaving contraction out of the total expansion picture.
- Letting one-time fees inflate recurring growth.
- Changing definitions between finance, product, and sales.
