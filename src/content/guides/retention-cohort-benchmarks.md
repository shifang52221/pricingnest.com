## What this page is for

Retention cohort benchmarks help the team compare current behavior with a stable baseline cohort. They are useful when
the business wants to know whether pricing, onboarding, or renewal changes are actually helping.

The goal is not to copy a benchmark number from somewhere else. The goal is to know whether this business's own
retention pattern is getting stronger or weaker over time.

## What to benchmark

Benchmark at least:

- month-1 retention
- month-3 retention
- NRR by cohort
- cohort behavior by segment

If the benchmark only looks at one average curve, it will hide meaningful differences between plans or customer types.

## Why it matters

Cohort benchmarks are one of the best ways to catch drift after a pricing or product change. They tell you whether a
new cohort is behaving differently from the baseline, and whether the difference is large enough to matter.

They are also a good check against overconfidence. A healthy headline metric can still hide a weak segment if the wrong
cohort was used as the benchmark.

## How to use the benchmarks

Select a stable baseline cohort, then compare new cohorts against it over several months. If the gap grows, the team
should investigate whether the issue is pricing, onboarding, plan fit, or value delivery.

Benchmarks are only useful when the cohort definition stays consistent. If the definition changes every month, the
benchmark is mostly noise.

## Common mistakes

- Changing cohort definitions every reporting period.
- Ignoring seasonality.
- Comparing new segments to legacy baselines without adjustment.
- Treating one benchmark curve as a universal target.
