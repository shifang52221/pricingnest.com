## What this page is for

Renewal forecasting turns renewal timing, cohort behavior, and billing cycle patterns into a forecast the business can
use for pricing and retention planning. It is not just an MRR projection. It is the model that tells you where renewal
risk will show up and how much it could matter.

## What to include in the forecast

Build the forecast around:

- cohort age
- plan type
- billing cycle
- expected churn
- expected contraction
- expected expansion

Leaving any of those out makes the forecast look simpler than it really is.

## Why it matters

Renewal forecasts help the team see whether the current pricing motion is stable enough to keep growing. They also help
the business distinguish between a temporary timing issue and a structural retention issue.

If annual and monthly customers are blended carelessly, the forecast can look clean while hiding very different risk
profiles.

## How to use the forecast

Use the forecast to estimate renewal MRR, likely churn impact, and where expansion might offset losses. Compare the
forecast with recent actuals every month so the model does not drift away from reality.

If the forecast starts to miss consistently, the issue is often not just the math. It can also be a change in customer
behavior, value delivery, or renewal process.

## Common mistakes

- Treating annual and monthly renewals as the same problem.
- Using last quarter averages without checking seasonality.
- Ignoring plan migrations near renewal dates.
- Forecasting without a review loop.
