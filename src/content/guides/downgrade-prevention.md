## What this page is for

Downgrade prevention is about contraction, not full churn. It helps the business reduce revenue loss caused by tier
downshifts, weaker value perception, or pricing changes that leave the middle tier poorly differentiated.

If the team treats downgrade pressure as generic churn, it may solve the wrong problem.

## What to diagnose first

Before trying to prevent downgrades, diagnose:

- whether the affected tier still feels worth paying for
- whether the lower tier is too close to the higher one
- whether price changes created a weaker value story
- whether the downgrade is happening at renewal or mid-cycle
- whether support or usage patterns point to a poor plan fit

Those factors tell you whether the fix is packaging, messaging, limit design, or renewal handling.

## Why it matters

Downgrades quietly erode recurring revenue even when churn looks stable. A business can report a healthy retention
headline while still leaking value through repeated contraction.

That is why downgrade analysis needs its own page and its own response path.

## How to respond

Measure downgrade rate by plan, then model the revenue loss. If the pattern is concentrated, the page structure or tier
fit may need work. If the pattern rises after price changes, the business may need stronger value proof or a cleaner
upgrade path.

The goal is not to eliminate every downgrade. The goal is to make sure customers only move down when the tier actually
fits their use case better.

## Common mistakes

- Treating downgrades as churn.
- Ignoring downgrade reasons in surveys or support data.
- Overlapping tier limits so heavily that the better plan is not obvious.
- Solving downgrade pressure with discounts instead of value clarity.
